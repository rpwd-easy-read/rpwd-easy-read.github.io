## S3 — Equality and non-discrimination

- You have the right to be treated equally to everyone else in
  every part of life.
- The government has to make this real by removing barriers and
  making places, services and information reach you.
- Someone can refuse you a service, a job, or a place only if they
  can show a real and serious reason, and that saying no is a fair
  way to reach that reason. The law calls this "a proportionate
  means of achieving a legitimate aim". Otherwise saying no is
  discrimination.
- No one can lock you up or take away your freedom only because
  you have a disability.
- If a small change would let you take part (a ramp, extra time, a
  different format, a support person), the government or the
  service has to make that change. The law calls this "reasonable
  accommodation".

## S4 — Women and children with disabilities

- Women with disabilities and children with disabilities have the
  same rights as everyone else, at home, at school, at work, in
  health, in courts, everywhere.
- The government and local authorities have to take measures so
  that rights reach women and children with disabilities equally.
- Children with disabilities have the right to share their views on
  anything that affects them.
- Adults have to listen to what the child says, and give the child
  the support they need to be heard, in a way that fits their age
  and their disability.

<!-- staff-note: cf. S4(1) equal enjoyment; S4(2) child's views and support. -->

## S5 — Community life

- You have the right to live in the community, in your own home or
  with your family or in a shared home you chose, like anyone else.
- No one can force you to live in one particular kind of place, for
  example a hostel or an institution, because of your disability.
- The government has to try to make community support available:
  help that comes to you at home, community-based homes, and
  personal assistance so you can live your own life.
- Support should fit your age and gender.

<!-- staff-note: cf. S5(1) right to community life; S5(2)(a) no obligation to any particular arrangement; S5(2)(b) in-house/residential/community services incl. personal assistance. -->

## S6 — Protection from cruelty and inhuman treatment

- No one can torture you, hurt you cruelly, or treat you in a way
  that lowers your dignity. The government has to protect you.
- You can never be part of a medical or scientific research study
  unless two things are true.
- One: you have understood what the study is about, in a form you
  can follow, and you have said yes freely.
- Two: a special committee has approved the study. At least half
  the people on that committee must themselves be persons with
  disabilities or belong to a disability organisation.

<!-- staff-note: cf. S6(1) torture/cruel treatment; S6(2)(i) free and informed consent in accessible modes; S6(2)(ii) Committee constitution requirement — "not less than half" persons with disabilities. -->

## S7 — Protection from abuse, violence and exploitation

- No one can abuse you, use force on you, or take advantage of you.
  The government has to protect you, prevent harm, rescue and help
  survivors, and spread awareness.
- You, or any group working for persons with disabilities, can tell
  the Executive Magistrate for the area if abuse is happening, has
  happened, or looks like it will happen.
- The Magistrate has to act quickly. They can order a rescue,
  order safe custody if you want it, or order money for your
  upkeep.
- If a police officer hears about the abuse, they have to tell you
  four things: your right to ask the Magistrate for protection;
  the nearest organisation that helps people with disabilities;
  your right to free legal aid; and your right to file a
  complaint.
- The police officer still has to investigate the crime under
  regular law, in addition to telling you your rights.
- If the Magistrate thinks a crime under other laws has been
  committed too, they send the complaint to the criminal court.

## S8 — Protection and safety in emergencies

- You have the same right to safety as anyone else in dangerous
  times: disasters like floods and earthquakes, fires, armed
  conflict, and emergencies.
- The National and State Disaster Management Authorities have to
  include persons with disabilities in their plans.
- The District Disaster Management Authority has to keep a record
  of persons with disabilities in the district and warn them when
  danger is coming.
- When buildings, roads, and services are rebuilt after a disaster
  or conflict, the work has to meet the accessibility needs of
  persons with disabilities. The rebuilding team has to consult
  the State Commissioner for Persons with Disabilities.

<!-- staff-note: cf. S8(1) equal protection; S8(2) NDMA/SDMA inclusion; S8(3) DDMA record-keeping and warning; S8(4) reconstruction accessibility with State Commissioner consultation. -->

## S9 — Home and family

- No child with a disability can be taken away from their parents
  only because the child has a disability, or only because the
  parent has a disability.
- A court can separate a child from parents only if the court
  decides it is in the child's best interest, and only through a
  proper court order.
- If the parents really cannot care for the child, the court has
  to try in this order: place the child with near relations
  first; then with a family setting in the community; and only in
  exceptional cases send the child to a shelter run by the
  government or an NGO.

<!-- staff-note: cf. S9(1) separation only by competent court in child's best interest; S9(2) placement priority — near relations → community family setting → shelter (exceptional). -->

## S10 — Reproductive rights

- You have the right to full and clear information about
  reproduction and family planning. The government has to make
  sure you get it.
- No doctor or hospital can perform any medical procedure on you
  that would stop you from having children unless you have
  understood it and said yes freely and knowingly.
- This means no forced sterilisation, whether by a family member,
  an institution, or a doctor.

<!-- staff-note: cf. S10(1) information duty on Government; S10(2) prohibition of infertility-causing procedure without free and informed consent. -->

## S11 — Voting

- The Election Commission of India and every State Election
  Commission have to make sure every polling station is
  accessible to persons with disabilities.
- All election material, ballots, notices, forms and
  instructions, has to be in a form you can understand and use.

<!-- staff-note: cf. S11 — one clause, two duties: physical access to polling stations, accessibility of electoral materials. -->

## S12 — Access to justice

- You have the right to go to any court, tribunal, commission, or
  authority that has legal or investigative powers, on an equal
  basis with others. Nothing about disability can block you.
- The government has to put support in place, especially for
  persons with disabilities living outside their families and
  those needing high support.
- The National and State Legal Services Authorities have to make
  their schemes and services usable by persons with disabilities,
  which includes reasonable accommodation.
- The government has to make three things happen inside the
  justice system. Public documents in accessible formats. The
  offices that file and store records equipped to work in
  accessible formats. Facilities to record your evidence, your
  arguments, and your views in the language and mode of
  communication you choose.

## S13 — Legal capacity

- You have the right to own things, inherit property, run your
  own money, and take bank loans and credit, on equal terms with
  anyone else.
- The government has to make sure you have the same standing
  before the law as any other person. This is what "legal
  capacity" means: the law counts your decisions.
- If someone is helping you decide, and their own interest
  clashes with what is best for you in a money or property
  matter, they have to step out of that particular decision.
- The law does not assume there is a clash just because your
  helper is a family member by blood, marriage, or adoption.
- You can change or stop a support arrangement whenever you want.
  You can pick a new helper. Deals you already made with other
  people, while that helper supported you, still stand.
- Anyone helping you with your decisions must not push you or
  force you, and has to respect what you want, who you are, and
  your privacy.

## S14 — Guardianship

- Guardianship is the last step. Support to make your own
  decisions has to be tried first.
- Only a district court, or an authority the State Government
  names, can decide you need a guardian, and only if support has
  been given and still could not enable you to take legally
  binding decisions.
- The court gives a "limited guardian": someone who takes
  legally binding decisions together with you. They must consult
  you.
- Limited guardianship is joint decision-making. It runs on
  trust. It is only for a specific time, for a specific decision
  or situation, and it has to follow what you want.
- The court can grant total support only in serious cases, or
  where limited guardianship is being granted again and again.
  Even then, the court has to review it and decide what kind of
  support is needed.
- Any guardian who was already appointed under an older law is
  now treated as a limited guardian from the day this Act came
  into force.
- If you do not agree with the authority's decision to appoint a
  guardian, you can appeal to a higher authority that the State
  Government names.

## S15 — Offices that support your decisions

- The government has to name one or more offices to build
  community awareness and support persons with disabilities to
  use their legal capacity, that is, to make their own decisions.
- These offices have to set up support arrangements so persons
  with disabilities can exercise their legal capacity. Two groups
  of people are specifically named: persons living in
  institutions, and persons who need high support.

<!-- staff-note: cf. S15(1) designation duty; S15(2) support arrangements especially for institutional residents and high-support users. -->
